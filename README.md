## Course Description

Why do people organize to change the world around them? Why do people sometimes protest but more often not? How do movements work, and why do some succeed while others fail?

At various points in history, social movements have had a substantial impact on social policy and politics. In the 1910s, women organized to earn their right to vote. In the 1960s, many protested the gross injustices endured by African-Americans in defense of civil rights. In the 1970s, college students, draft-dodgers, and others worked together to oppose the Vietnam War. In the late 2000s, a Tea Party movement emerged to protest the government's involvement in the redistribution of wealth. As these examples show, social movements are critical to American democracy.

This course explores the origins, dynamics, and consequences of social movements, examining topics including the emergence of movements, recruitment, interactions between movements and the public or political officials, tactics, and the factors contributing to movement success and failure.


## Course Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

1. Introduce the concept of social movements and how it differs from collective behavior
2. Examine the emergence, development, dynamics, tactics, and impacts of social movements in the United States
3. Gain familiarity with current research on social movements
4. Apply social movement theories to contemporary issues
5. Develop an action plan for organization-building around a contemporary issue

## Assignments

### Course Preparation Module (40 pts)
Completed during Week 1. Includes verifying the email policy, setting up Google Doc submissions, installing Zotero, updating your course profile, and posting a video introduction on the class discussion board.

### Course Engagement (50 pts)
Active engagement with course materials is critical for success. Engagement is assessed through participation in discussions and consistent access to online course content.

### Discussion Leader (40 pts)
Twice during the semester, students work in a team to lead class discussion on an assigned article or reading.

### "Building a Movement" Playbook (25 pts)
In a group, students design a plan for a hypothetical social movement organization focused on a contemporary social issue. The playbook covers the group's name, grievances, goals, and structure; strategies for gaining resources and participants; tactics for affecting policy change; and potential responses to sanctions, repression, or counter-mobilization.

### "Building a Movement" Presentation (25 pts)
In a group, students present the key points outlined in their movement playbook.

### "Building a Movement" Reflection (20 pts)
A 1-page (single-spaced) reflection on the potential outcomes for their organization, its effectiveness, potential internal and external problems, and their broader consequences.
